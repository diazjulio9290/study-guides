01What is Artificial Intelligence?

Definition

A broad field of study focused on enabling computers to perform tasks that normally require human-level intelligence. Dates back to the 1950s with games like tic-tac-toe and checkers.

Key AI Application Areas

Chatbots & virtual assistants — Content generation — Translation — Image recognition — Recommendation systems — Spam filters — Navigation — Fraud detection

02Evolution of AI & Machine Learning
1950s
Early AI
Early applications in games (tic-tac-toe, checkers). Limited practical use for decades. Inspired science fiction.
1980s
Machine Learning Emerges
Statistical techniques to create models from observed data. Human-defined feature extractors. Examples: linear regression, bag-of-words, spam filters.
2010s
Deep Learning Breakthrough
Advances in hardware, large datasets, and improved training algorithms. Automated feature extraction via deep neural networks (DNNs). GPU-enabled large-scale training.
Now
Generative AI & LLMs
Large language models with surprisingly human-like intelligence. Transforming industries. Agentic AI — autonomous agents forming a $100B+ software category.

04Training vs. Inference

Model Training

  • Most computationally intensive phase
  • Expose model to vast labeled datasets over time
  • Automated feature extraction via DNNs
  • Iterative validation & testing
  • Time-consuming and expensive on CPUs
  • GPU acceleration is critical
  • Example: Showing 1000s of cat images
VS

Inference

  • Applying a trained model to new unseen data
  • Generating output / predictions
  • Less compute-intensive than training overall
  • Must be fast, at scale, often real-time
  • Delays are unacceptable (e.g. navigation)
  • GPU acceleration also essential here
  • Example: Recognizing a new cat photo

06How GPUs Address AI Pipeline Bottlenecks

The Problem: CPU Bottlenecks

CPU-based workflows hit performance walls when handling large-scale AI workloads. Not designed for the massive parallel data processing required by deep learning.

GPU Training Acceleration

Thousands of parallel cores handle matrix math simultaneously. Reduces training time from weeks to hours. Enables iterative experimentation at scale.

GPU Inference at Scale

Real-time predictions require immediate response. GPUs process inference requests rapidly and at massive scale — essential for production AI deployments.

07AI Deployment Challenges
⚙️
Complexity

Multiple interconnected components in a data pipeline. Maintaining stable platforms for both development and deployment is challenging.

🏭
Production Readiness

Organizations must consider cost, manageability, security, and maintenance when moving AI solutions into production environments.

🌐
Infrastructure Diversity

Running AI workflows across data centers, edge, and cloud is not guaranteed. Siloed infrastructure increases costs and operational challenges.

09Key Vocabulary
TermDefinition
Machine LearningA subset of AI using statistical techniques to build models from observed data; emerged in the 1980s.
Deep Neural Network (DNN)Multi-layered networks that automate feature extraction; backbone of modern AI breakthroughs in the 2010s.
Generative AIAI systems capable of generating new content (text, images, code) with near-human quality.
Large Language Model (LLM)Massive DNN trained on text data; powers chatbots, translation, and content generation.
Model TrainingExposing a model to large datasets iteratively to learn patterns. The most GPU-intensive AI phase.
InferenceUsing a trained model to generate predictions on new, unseen data. Must be fast and scalable.
Agentic AIAutonomous AI agents that complete complex tasks independently, forming a $100B+ market by 2030.
GPU AccelerationUsing GPU parallel cores to overcome CPU bottlenecks in AI training and inference workflows.
Feature ExtractorA component that identifies meaningful patterns in data. Originally human-defined; now automated by DNNs.
HPCHigh-Performance Computing — massively exceeds regular server performance via distributed GPU compute.
10Summary & Takeaways

What You Need to Know

  • AI is a broad field enabling computers to perform human-level tasks; ML and deep learning are key subsets.
  • Deep learning breakthroughs in the 2010s were enabled by GPUs, large datasets, and better algorithms.
  • Generative AI and LLMs now power chatbots, content generation, translation, and more.
  • Agentic AI is the next wave — autonomous agents that transform enterprises into digital workforces.
  • AI delivers measurable ROI: 30–50% cost savings in finance, 30% satisfaction in healthcare.
  • Model training is the most compute-intensive phase; GPUs reduce training time dramatically.
  • Inference must be real-time and at scale — GPU acceleration is equally essential here.
  • A typical AI workflow has 7 steps: Acquisition → Curation → Pre-training → Customization → Retrieval → Inference → Guardrails.
  • Key deployment challenges: complexity, production readiness, infrastructure diversity.
  • GPUs can be deployed on-premises, in the cloud, or co-location facilities to support AI workloads.
